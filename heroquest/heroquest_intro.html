HeroQuest the boardgame

Imagine diving into a dungeon with your friends—one of you becomes the evil Zargon, the others are mighty heroes: a wizard, a barbarian, an elf, and a dwarf. Welcome to HeroQuest, the tabletop game of cooperative exploration, danger, and treasure.

What is HeroQuest?

HeroQuest is a dungeon-crawling board game originally released in the late 1980s by Milton Bradley and Games Workshop. It blends the strategy of roleplaying games like Dungeons & Dragons with the accessibility and tactile charm of board games. One player becomes the Game Master—called Zargon—while the rest are heroes adventuring together through a series of quests.

Think of it like a fantasy movie come to life on your table—every room explored is a scene, every enemy a cinematic battle. It's collaborative storytelling through dice, miniatures, and imagination.

Roles in HeroQuest

There are five key roles in HeroQuest: Zargon (the villainous game master), and four heroes: Barbarian, Dwarf, Elf, and Wizard. Each has strengths and weaknesses—like characters in a video game RPG.

Exploring the Dungeon: Real-World Analogy

Picture HeroQuest like exploring an escape room—but the room is magical, filled with goblins, and you have a sword. Zargon is the designer, creating secret puzzles and revealing them only when players make choices. The dungeon map begins hidden and is revealed room by room.

Each square in the dungeon represents a moment of choice. Will you walk forward and trigger a trap? Will a monster be hiding behind that door?

Dice Mechanics and Movement

HeroQuest uses custom dice and movement rules. You roll two six-sided dice to determine how many squares you can move. This randomness is similar to rolling a die in Monopoly, but here it creates tension—can you reach the treasure chest before the orc does?

Why It’s Still Popular

HeroQuest isn’t just nostalgic—it’s a gateway game. It teaches you storytelling, cooperation, tactics, and creativity. It paved the way for modern board games like Gloomhaven, Descent, and Dungeons & Dragons board game series.

Much like LEGO lets you build anything, HeroQuest lets you build stories. The pieces may be plastic, but the adventures are real.

Summary Metaphor: Dungeon Crawling as Movie Directing

Think of HeroQuest like being in a fantasy film:

  • Zargon is the director and scriptwriter.
  • The players are the actors improvising scenes.
  • The board is the stage, set one room at a time.
  • Dice are the element of chance—your special effects budget.
